7. Tuples have no field access and no nested payload patterns. `t.0` is a parse error ("expected a field name after `.`"), and `@some((a, b))` type-checks but fails to lower with "variant `some`'s payload pattern needs a non-wildcard sub-pattern ... only plain bindings (`@some(x)`) are supported". Together these mean a tuple can only be taken apart by a `let (a, b) = t` in a statement position, so every consumer of `zip`/`enumerate` must bind the pair whole and destructure it on the next line. This is the R3a gap from the design, slightly wider than recorded there: the design notes `for (k, v)` fails, but the same limitation applies to any pattern nesting a tuple, and `.0` closes off the other obvious spelling. Every map iterator in the collections design yields tuples, so this sits on the critical path.
8. A module-qualified struct literal does not parse. `q.holder { value: 7 }` after `use p.q` reports "expected end of line after this" pointing at `holder`, so the brace form is only reachable through an unqualified name. The *type* path (`q.holder` in an annotation) resolves, and a member import (`use p.q.holder`) gives a working spelling, so this is a parser gap rather than a resolution one — `parse_postfix` does not consider a `{` after a dotted path to open a struct literal. Found while fixing the qualified-type-path gap; the two are adjacent but independent.
